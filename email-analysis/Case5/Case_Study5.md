# Case Study 5 — MK Wholesaler Multi‑Role Remote Job Recruitment Scam (Employment Fraud Attempt)

## Overview
This case study documents a fraudulent recruitment attempt impersonating a non‑existent employer called **“MK Wholesaler.”** The attacker used a Gmail address, unrealistic salary offers, vague job descriptions, and a malicious Microsoft Teams workflow to lure victims into direct contact with a fake “HR Manager.” The goal was likely identity theft, financial fraud, or malware delivery.

This analysis demonstrates my ability to identify social‑engineering patterns, evaluate sender legitimacy, and document high‑risk employment scams.

## Red Flags Identified
Below are the indicators that confirmed this was a fraudulent employment scam.

A. Gmail Sender Address
The email originated from:

ashleytoney895@gmail.com

Legitimate companies do not recruit through personal Gmail accounts.
This is the strongest indicator of fraud.

B. Non‑Existent Company (“MK Wholesaler”)
No legitimate business named MK Wholesaler exists:
No website
No corporate domain
No LinkedIn presence
No public records

Scammers often invent generic business names to appear legitimate.

C. Unrealistic Salary Offers
The attacker listed 10+ roles with highly inflated hourly wages:
Data Entry — $27.18/hr
Customer Service — $29.12/hr
Logistics Manager — $42.10/hr
Accounting — $47.8/hr
Software Developer — $49.2/hr

These numbers are:
too precise
too high
too uniform
too convenient

Inflated salaries are used to lure victims quickly.

D. No Job Descriptions or Application Process
The email provides:
no job posting
no job details
no responsibilities
no qualifications
no resume review
no hiring timeline

Legitimate companies do not skip these steps.

E. Suspicious Microsoft Teams Workflow
The attacker instructs the victim to:
Click a Teams invite link
Add a “HR Manager”
Message her directly
Provide a reference code

This workflow is not used by legitimate employers.

It is commonly used in:
identity theft
fake onboarding scams
check fraud
remote‑equipment scams
cryptocurrency laundering schemes

F. Generic Greeting (“Dear Candidate”)
Legitimate recruiters:
address candidates by name
reference the job applied for
reference the application source
Scammers use generic greetings to mass‑send emails.

G. No Corporate Signature Block
The email lacks:
company address
phone number
website
HR contact information
recruiter identity verification
This is typical of scam communications.

H. Vague, Inconsistent Writing Style
Indicators include:
unnatural salary precision
inconsistent formatting
vague job titles
no punctuation consistency
no branding
no professionalism

This matches known scam templates.

## Likely Objectives of the Scam
Based on the structure and content, the attacker likely intended to:

Harvest personal information
Collect identity documents (ID, SSN, banking info)
Deliver malware via Teams or follow‑up files
Initiate a fake check or equipment‑purchase scam
Move the victim into a private communication channel

This all aligns with modern employment‑fraud patterns.

---

## Final Assessment
This communication is conclusively identified as a fraudulent employment scam involving:

Gmail‑based impersonation
Fake company identity
Unrealistic salary offers
Malicious Teams workflow
Social engineering
High‑risk fraud indicators

No legitimate employer would contact candidates in this manner.

---

## Recommended Defensive Actions
- Do not click the Teams link
- Block the sender
- Report the email to Outlook’s phishing system
- Document the incident (this case study)
- Monitor for similar patterns in future recruitment scams

---

## Conclusion
This case demonstrates a high‑risk employment scam using a multi‑role job lure, inflated salaries, and a malicious Teams workflow. The attacker’s intent was likely identity theft or financial fraud. Early detection prevented exposure to further harm.
